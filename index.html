What Are the Oil Sands?

The Alberta oil sands are one of the largest industrial projects on Earth. But their expansion comes at a great cost to the environment and to Indigenous communities whose lands are being poisoned.

Bitumen extraction is extremely toxic — releasing pollutants into rivers, poisoning ecosystems, and making traditional food sources unsafe to eat.

This industrial development is happening near Fort McMurray, in the heart of Treaty 8 territory, close to many Indigenous communities.

Environmental and Human Impacts

Air, Water, and Land

Extracting bitumen releases greenhouse gases and poisons water systems like the Athabasca River. Wildlife and fish are dying, and smoke stacks pollute the air people breathe.

Health and Culture

People in Fort Chipewyan and surrounding areas have reported higher cancer rates. Hunting, trapping, and fishing — vital cultural practices — are becoming impossible.

Focus on the Athabasca Chipewyan First Nation (ACFN)

The ACFN have seen their traditional territory eroded by oil development. Their hunting, fishing, and trapping areas are shrinking or poisoned.

Chief Allan Adam said: “Industry comes in, makes the mess, and leaves us to live with it.”

ACFN continues to challenge the government and industry for violating their treaty rights and damaging their homeland.

Melina Laboucan-Massimo

Melina is from the Lubicon Cree Nation and is a powerful voice for climate justice. She has worked with Greenpeace and Indigenous Climate Action (ICA).

She advocates for a just transition and emphasizes the importance of Indigenous consent and leadership in environmental decisions.

Treaty Rights & Legal Issues

The oil sands lie on Treaty 8 territory. Under Canadian law and the United Nations Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples (UNDRIP), Indigenous peoples must give free, prior, and informed consent to resource projects.

Yet, many projects move ahead without proper consultation. Groups like ACFN continue to fight these violations in court and on the land.
